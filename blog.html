Reflecting on the Program

By Rafe Burns · April 2026

How I got here

When I was a senior in high school during the COVID-19 pandemic, I read the famous book Moneyball, which detailed how the Oakland Athletics baseball team used data analytics to build the most cost-efficient and competitive baseball team they could with the limited resources they were afforded as a small market team. That inspired me to start looking into data analytics as a potential area to study in college. Once I found out that Syracuse had a dedicated major in Sport Analytics, I knew that it was the place for me. Early on in my freshman year, a peer advisor described the track he followed during his time at Syracuse and mentioned how he had graduated with an undergraduate degree early and then went on to study in the iSchool in the Applied Data Science program. From that point forward, I used his academic path as a goal for myself: attempting to graduate with the Sport Analytics degree early and then pursuing a master’s degree. I was fortunate to have achieved this goal, and even outperformed my expectations, adding in a BA in Economics and a CAS in Cloud Management along the way.

Through the sport analytics undergraduate program, I felt that I developed a strong baseline and foundation of data science skills. I had gotten a lot of exposure to machine learning, which I have now learned is rare for an undergraduate curriculum. I was also able to learn how to conduct end-to-end research projects; I entered several data competitions and hackathons revolving around sports which allowed me to gain exposure to real-world datasets and receive feedback from industry professionals on the quality of my analyses.

While earning a master’s degree in applied data science was on the path I envisioned for myself since my freshman year, I ultimately decided to pursue it because I wanted to continue to learn more about data science and build my skillset even more. I felt that the career and specific domain I wanted to go after was as a machine learning engineer, and therefore after researching some of the classes the program offered, I believed that it would properly prepare me for those types of jobs. Additionally, I had been lightly exposed to topics such as deep learning and natural language processing, and I felt that I would be a step ahead of my peers entering the workforce if I had studied these topics some more. Another reason I decided to get a master’s in applied data science was because I felt that my previous experience with data was almost too confined to just the sport analytics space, and I wanted to open myself up to other industries, such as banking, healthcare, and entertainment.

My track

The track I chose to concentrate on within the Applied Data Science program was Artificial Intelligence. I am a firm believer that AI is the future, and we can see it in all facets of life. I originally felt like I wanted to do the Business and Data Analytics track, as I thought that it more closely aligned with the type of work I had done in my undergraduate degrees, Sport Analytics and Economics. However, after doing some internships and independent projects, I found myself more drawn to the classes in the AI program. I knew that in the future I wanted to do something along the lines of machine learning and predictive analytics, and I believed that the AI track offered classes that would help deepen my understanding in this area.

The two classes I took to meet the track’s requirements were Deep Learning in Practice and Natural Language Processing. I really enjoyed the deep learning class because I found a lot of real-world applications for every topic covered throughout the semester. Furthermore, I’ve already implemented the same things I learned in some other independent projects. For example, I tried to build a model using RNNs and Seq2Seq forecasting to predict prices of cryptocurrencies. Additionally, I’ve done some work using reinforcement learning in the field of sport analytics for strategic game planning in curling. With my Natural Language Processing class, I learned a lot about how natural language understanding and generation work, along with semantic word vectors. One of the models I learned about was BERT, which I have attempted to use to predict March Madness Tournament outcomes. Therefore, I believe my expectations of the learning outcomes for this track were more than satisfied.

I also am pursuing a Certificate of Advanced Study in Cloud Data Management. I decided to add this CAS because all the industry professionals I talked to told me how valuable it was to have not only an understanding of SQL and databases, but also cloud systems like AWS or Azure. Through these classes, like Cloud Management and Data Warehousing, I have gotten exposure to these tools and been able to get hands-on experience with them.

Classes

Spring 2025

Over the course of the program, I took 12 classes, 4 per semester. My first term, in the spring of 2025, I took IST 687 – Introduction to Data Science. This class largely served as a baseline to make sure all the students in the program were starting at the same level when it came to data science. Almost all of the class involved learning how to use the programming language R, which I was lucky enough to have used for most of my undergraduate studies. However, even though I had already built up this skill, I was still able to take away several key concepts. For example, the machine learning I had learned in the Sport Analytics program included models like Random Forest, LightGBM, and XGBoost, but the curriculum skipped over some simpler and often times more effective models such as Support Vector Machines. I found SVMs to be very easy to understand and I think that not only are they simple but they often are able to outperform some of the more complex algorithms at certain tasks. Another topic I learned, which was probably my favorite from this class, was Association Rules Mining. I had never heard of the idea beforehand, but I think that it is incredibly powerful in identifying trends and relationships between objects; I’ve gone on to use it in internships with real-world datasets.

Another class I took this first semester was IST 659 – Data Administration Concepts and Database Management. This class revolved around building up my SQL skills. Similar to how I had experience with R prior to IST 687, I had some experience with SQL in my undergraduate classes, but I was far from confident in my abilities to write a query more complex than a simple SELECT statement. While taking this class, I learned about joins, views, and most importantly: properly designing a database system. For the final project in this class, I built out a database for a hospital system to track their doctors, patients, and ambulances; I did this both through actually writing out SQL statements and planning out the database with things like entity relationship diagrams. As I mentioned earlier, many industry professionals have suggested to me that I work on my SQL skills, as they saw this as the most important skill for a data scientist to have. Therefore, I am very glad that I was able to really improve my knowledge of SQL through this class and have applied what I learned to internships.

The other classes I took during this semester were IST 686 – Quantitative Reasoning for Data Science, and MBC 638 – Data Analysis and Decision Making. IST 686 was primarily a statistics course, where I learned to interpret models, particularly looking at calculating things like F statistics and P values or examining null/alternative hypotheses. The class used all R which again helped me build my skillset in this language. For the final project in this class, I found a dataset about the health of newborn babies and ran a series of tests looking at the relationship between a mother’s behavior (smoking/drinking during pregnancy) and birthing outcomes (premature births, size of baby, etc.). MBC 638, which was offered by the Whitman School, mostly used Excel. I had been using Excel since I was in middle school, but I was able to learn a lot about some advanced functions and data analysis features that the platform offered. I was also able to receive a certification of Mastery in Microsoft Excel through this class, which is something that employers look for.

Fall 2025

My second semester in the program, the fall of 2025, was likely the most difficult set of classes I took during the three semesters, however I felt that it is the time in which I met my goals of learning more about machine learning and deep learning. I took IST 691 – Deep Learning in Practice, which covered some pretty advanced concepts. Through this class, I learned about all types of neural networks, including GNNs, RNNs, and CNNs; furthermore, I also enjoyed learning about reinforcement learning. My favorite topic covered in this class was definitely the RNNs (Recurrent Neural Networks), as they deal largely with time series data, which is something that I have run into a lot when conducting my own independent research and in hackathon competitions. Since taking this class, I have applied nearly everything I learned to some sort of project. For example, I am an avid rugby fan, and was able to acquire a dataset that included the location of players on the field at every timestamp over the course of a game; I then used an RNN and temporal difference learning to look at sequences of events to determine which ones most contributed to a team scoring on that possession. I have also combined my knowledge of game theory economics with reinforcement learning to build models to optimize pitch sequencing in baseball. Additionally, my final project in this class revolved around the idea of building an indie music recommendation system; I did this by first converting audio into spectrograms that I could then pass through a convolutional autoencoder to produce embeddings. I then mapped these embeddings in a latent space to find similar songs.

Another class I took this semester was IST 707 – Applied Machine Learning. Similar to IST 691, taking this class aligned with the main reason I decided to pursue the applied data science master’s degree. As I previously mentioned, I had a pretty strong foundation in machine learning from the sport analytics program, however this class built on a lot of the areas that I did not learn in my undergraduate studies. For example, I had been self-taught in the area of clustering, but this class taught me all types of mechanisms like DBSCAN, HDBSCAN, and K-Means. Another way this class built on my foundation was through studying hyperparameters of models and their importance. When building models prior to this class, I would really only touch a few hyperparameters, but wouldn’t give much attention to them and would instead focus solely on feature engineering. However, I have now learned the importance of tuning hyperparameters, and also learned different optimization methods, such as grid search, to build the best possible models. For the final project in this class, I did a complete analysis of a mental health survey collected by the Barnes Center, aimed at identifying students who are at risk of experiencing depression and anxiety. I first trained random forest models based on several questions from the survey relating to behavioral patterns to predict whether someone was going through mental health struggles. I then isolated the most important features from these models and used various clustering methods to build groups and profiles of students on campus; some of the identified clusters turned out to be highly populated with students that showed signs of depression or anxiety. We felt that the Barnes Center could use our analysis when conducting targeted outreach to certain demographics, informing them of therapy or counseling sessions that may benefit certain students.

I also took IST 664 – Natural Language Processing during this semester. As time goes on, I hear more and more about NLP and how it is being incorporated into all aspects of daily life. Therefore, I am glad that I was able to take a class dedicated to it, as I learned more about what goes on under the hood in models produced by OpenAI or Anthropic. I’ve applied what I learned here in some creative ways; for example, I built a BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) model to predict March Madness college basketball outcomes for a Kaggle competition. I constructed a set of text for every possible matchup with team statistics and game information, hoping that the BERT model would pick up trends on how certain teams historically perform in the tournament. The last class I took this semester was IST 722 – Data Warehousing. I chose to take this class because of what industry professionals had told me about SQL and cloud skills. Through this class, I learned all about data warehouse models such as DBT and Snowflake. I had heard a lot about Snowflake and see it as a requirement in job postings, so I am glad that I was able to get some experience using the tool. For the final project in this class, I designed and implemented a database model for movies and their cast, production crew, and reviews.

Spring 2026

During my last semester in the program, the spring of 2026, I took several classes that satisfied the requirements of the Cloud Management Certificate of Advanced Studies. One of which is IST 615 – Cloud Management. In this class, I learned all about cloud-based systems, including Microsoft’s Azure and Amazon’s AWS. I think these are crucial systems to understand, as they are important pieces of infrastructure when deploying an interface or web app. I personally use Azure for a startup business I have, and it is a very overwhelming tool at first as there are hundreds, if not thousands, of resources and capabilities. Through this class I learned how to navigate it and it has greatly improved the efficiency and functional ceiling of the startup. Similar to Azure, I have learned the same kind of skills with AWS, which I believe is more common in the workplace and in practice. For the final project in this class, I built a backend hosted on an Azure web app that is capable of taking in an uploaded CSV, and then building a series of models, including linear regression, random forest, and XGBoost. The finalized models can then be exported as a pkl file and used in whatever deployment the user desires.

Another cloud-based class I took in the spring 2026 semester is IST 769 – Advanced Big Data Management. Prior to this class, I really only had experience using the simplest forms of SQL through SQL Server. However, I have now been exposed to a variety of database management systems that are used in the real world. These include systems like Hadoop, Graph databases, ElasticSearch, and Kafka. One thing I appreciated about this class was that every time I was introduced to a new system, I learned about how it is deployed in the real world, such as the systems that large companies like Netflix and Uber use. As I mentioned earlier, I am involved with a startup, and I have taken what I learned through this class and applied it to the database models we use in production. Another key skill I learned in this class was querying a database using Python with Spark and Drill. This is a skill that I have discovered is highly sought after by employers and I am glad that I have gotten some experience with it. For my final project in this class, I will be using ClickHouse to make an NBA database that is easily queryable.

I also am enrolled in IST 652 – Scripting for Data Analysis this semester. Through this class I am learning how to essentially extract data from various sources and in different forms (structured, semi-structured, and unstructured) and then store it. Some of the ways that I have learned to acquire data are through web scraping and calling APIs. Similar to the other classes I have taken this semester, I have found concepts from this class that I can apply to my startup. For example, building API endpoints is a huge takeaway for me. Additionally, I have been able to explore some other aspects of data science through this class; I completed an advanced topic presentation on CatBoost, a machine learning algorithm built for datasets with an abundance of categorical features. The last class I took this semester is SCM 651 – Business Analytics. I feel that a lot of this class has built off of things from other classes: the basics of R from IST 687 and Excel from MBC 638. However, one massive thing that I am learning in this class is the importance of visualizations, particularly with Tableau and Power BI. These are two tools that I often see on job listings and I am glad that I have had the chance to properly learn how to use these powerful tools.

Internships and external projects

I was able to complete a few really interesting and meaningful internships during my time in the Applied Data Science Master’s program. This past summer I completed an internship as a Business Strategy & Analytics intern with DC United, a professional soccer team. Through the internship I was able to complete several projects that tied closely to what I learned through my studies. For example, I did one project with building models to predict the likelihood that members of their fanbase would purchase season tickets so that they could better optimize their outreach strategies; I took what I learned in IST 707 to build several machine learning models. I also was able to look into different segments of their fanbase to see trends in interests; to do this I applied the association rules mining I learned in IST 687.

I also completed an internship with EV Analytics last summer, an independent sports betting company that provides handicappers with odds and insights. The majority of my tasks here revolved around building models for college football betting markets. Again, I relied heavily on the techniques I learned in IST 707. I also was able to join the NEXIS Student Technology Lab during my time in the iSchool. Through this club, I was able to conduct some independent research on cryptocurrency markets and the trading strategy of scalping, where traders hold onto an asset for at most 2 minutes. I used several deep learning models that I learned in IST 691, particularly RNNs and Seq2Seq predictions. I was really excited to see that I was able to actually develop some profitable strategies for purchasing and selling Bitcoin and Ethereum at a profit.

Looking ahead

One thing that surprised me about the program was how much creativity it afforded me — both in how I approached data problems and in the projects I was able to pursue. Rather than following a rigid formula, I was encouraged to bring my own interests into my work, whether that was applying reinforcement learning to curling strategy or building a music recommendation system from raw audio files. My favorite class was IST 691 – Deep Learning in Practice, largely because of this freedom. Every topic we covered felt like it opened a door to a new project idea, and I found myself applying what I learned almost immediately to independent research and side projects outside of the classroom.

Looking back, the Applied Data Science program made me a significantly more well-rounded data scientist than I was when I started. I entered with a strong foundation in machine learning from my undergraduate work in Sport Analytics, but I left with a much broader and deeper skill set that now spans deep learning, natural language processing, cloud infrastructure, database design, and data engineering. More importantly, I learned how to connect these skills together — building end-to-end pipelines that go from raw data all the way through to a deployed model or interactive application. I feel confident that the technical abilities and problem-solving mindset I developed over these three semesters have prepared me well for the next chapter of my career.
