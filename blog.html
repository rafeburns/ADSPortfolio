Reflecting on the Program

A long-form look back at what I expected, what I actually learned, and what surprised me along the way.

By Rafe Burns · [PUBLICATION DATE] · ~3,000 words

Drafting tip. Aim for ~3,000 words total. The headings below match the prompts in the IST 782 rubric — you can keep, reorder, or rename them. Each section is sized roughly proportional to a 3,000-word target. Replace every [bracketed] block with your own writing.

What I expected to learn

[~300 words. When you applied to the program, what did you imagine "applied data science" was? What kind of work did you picture yourself doing? What tools did you assume you'd master? Set the baseline so the reader can see how your understanding evolved.]

What I actually learned

[Open this section with a short framing paragraph (~100 words) that compares your initial expectation with reality. Then walk through each of the six learning outcomes below.]

Outcome 1 — Collecting, storing, and accessing data

[~200 words. Which course(s) and assignments forced you to think about where data lives and how to get to it? What changed in how you approach a new dataset now versus before the program?]

Outcome 2 — Creating actionable insight across contexts

[~200 words. Talk about the full data science life cycle — problem framing, cleaning, modeling, interpretation. Where did you first see a project go all the way from question to recommendation?]

Outcome 3 — Visualization and predictive models

[~200 words. Which visualization or modeling technique surprised you with how powerful (or how limited) it was? When did you first feel confident picking the right model for a problem?]

Outcome 4 — Programming in R and Python

[~200 words. Where did your coding ability start, and where is it now? Did one language click for you faster than the other? What's a habit (notebooks, version control, testing) that you didn't have at the start of the program but rely on now?]

Outcome 5 — Communicating to broad audiences

[~200 words. Talk about a moment when you had to translate something technical for a non-technical audience — a sponsor, a manager, a classmate from a different background. What worked? What didn't?]

Outcome 6 — Ethics in data and predictive models

[~200 words. Was there a project, reading, or class discussion that changed how you think about fairness, bias, or privacy? What's a question you now ask yourself before starting any analysis that you didn't ask before?]

Work outside the classroom

[~200 words. Did you do an internship, an iConsult engagement, a Kaggle competition, a personal project, or any other work outside required coursework? If so, briefly describe it and how it complemented (or challenged) what you learned in class. If not, you can replace this section with a short note about how you supplemented your learning — reading, side projects, communities, conferences.]

My favorite class

[~250 words. Name the class. Then explain why — was it the instructor, the material, the projects, the people, the way it changed how you think? Be specific. The reader should be able to picture it.]

The best part of the program — and the biggest surprises

[~250 words. What's the single best thing you got out of the program (it doesn't have to be technical — could be a network, a piece of feedback, a mindset shift)? And what genuinely surprised you — something you wouldn't have predicted on day one?]

What's next

[~150 words. Optional but a nice closer. Where are you taking these skills next? What kind of problems do you want to work on?]
